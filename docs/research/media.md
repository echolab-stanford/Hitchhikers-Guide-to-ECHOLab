# Speaking with the Media About Your Research

## Overview

Media interviews are different from academic presentations. Reporters are often looking for clear explanations, memorable quotes, practical implications, and connections to ongoing public discussions.

The goal is not to simplify away the science. The goal is to communicate the science clearly and accurately.

## 1. Prepare Key Messages

Before an interview:

* Prepare a one- or two-sentence summary of the paper.
* Identify 3–5 key points you want to communicate.
* Prepare a few memorable statistics that help convey the magnitude of the findings.
* Review any accompanying commentary, editorial, or perspective article.

## 2. Think About the Quotes You Want Used

Only a small fraction of an interview typically appears in the final story.

* Think about one or two statements you would be comfortable seeing quoted verbatim.
* Reporters often look for concise explanations of why a result matters.
* Clear, memorable statements are more likely to appear in coverage than long, heavily qualified responses.

## 3. Anticipate Questions Beyond the Paper

Many questions will not be about the specific analyses in your study.

Common examples include:

* Why should people care?
* What should people do with this information?
* What policies should be implemented?
* How does this compare with previous research?
* What are the study's limitations?

## 4. Understand What Reporters Are Looking For

Researchers often focus on estimating effects or quantifying risks. Reporters and readers are often interested in a different question:

> What should be done with this information?

For example:

* A wildfire smoke study may lead readers to ask how they can protect themselves.
* A climate-economics study may lead readers to ask what businesses or governments should do in response.

Much of environmental health research quantifies risk, but reporters and readers want to know how to respond to that risk. The answer may not come from the paper itself, but it is often the most important part of the interview.

## 5. Distinguish Between the Paper and the Evidence Base

Reporters will often ask questions that extend beyond the specific analyses in your paper.

A useful distinction is between:

* What was directly examined in the paper.
* What is supported by the broader body of evidence.

For example, a wildfire smoke study may not evaluate protective actions, but researchers can still discuss recommendations from public health agencies, clinicians, intervention studies, or other relevant evidence.

Useful phrases include:

* "Our study found..."
* "Based on the broader evidence, experts generally recommend..."

## 6. Prepare Useful Numbers

Specific statistics can make findings easier to understand and remember.

Consider preparing:

* Key effect sizes.
* Population impacts.
* Comparisons that provide context.
* Additional descriptive statistics that may help communicate results to non-specialists.

## 7. Bridge Back to Your Main Message

You do not need to answer every question exactly as it is asked.

It is often useful to briefly acknowledge a question and then transition back to the main point you want readers to remember.

Examples:

* "That's an important question. What our study specifically found was..."
* "While our study did not directly examine that issue, one important finding from our work is..."
* "The main takeaway from our study is..."

Most readers will remember one or two messages from an interview. Repetition can be helpful.

## 8. Expect Simplification

News stories have much less space than scientific papers.

* Important nuances may be omitted.
* Findings may be simplified.
* Results may be framed differently than they are in the paper.

The goal is to communicate clearly while minimizing opportunities for misunderstanding.

## 9. Learn From Early Coverage

After the first few interviews:

* Identify questions reporters repeatedly ask.
* Look for common misunderstandings.
* Observe how different outlets frame the story.
* Review expert commentary and outside perspectives.

Good reporters often introduce context and ideas that can help improve future interviews.

## 10. Remember That Media Ecosystems Are Connected

Many reporters read previous coverage when reporting on a study.

* Early framing can influence later stories.
* Misunderstandings can spread across outlets.
* Clear communication early in the process can have outsized effects.

## Final Thought

A useful goal is to be able to explain your paper to an interested non-expert in one or two minutes.

If someone understands the main finding, why it matters, what decisions it might inform, and what its limitations are, the interview has likely been successful.
