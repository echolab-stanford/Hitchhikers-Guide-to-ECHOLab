# Research Workflows

## Overview

ECHOLab researchers use a variety of tools, programming languages, and analytical approaches. The goal of this guide is not to prescribe a single workflow, but to establish shared practices that make projects easier to find, understand, maintain, and hand off over time.

Our guiding principle is:

> Standardize interfaces, not workflows.

Researchers should have flexibility in how they conduct analyses and organize their day-to-day work. However, projects should provide a consistent set of entry points that allow collaborators and future researchers to understand:

- What the project is
- Where project resources are located
- Where data are stored
- Where manuscripts are maintained
- Who to contact with questions

A key goal is that a new collaborator should be able to get up to speed on a project quickly through project documentation and know where to find the resources needed to understand, reproduce, and extend prior work.

Project documentation also helps preserve continuity as researchers join, leave, and transition between roles. Some research resources and storage systems may not remain accessible after a researcher leaves the lab or Stanford, making clear project documentation and shared project repositories especially important.

Good project organization improves discoverability, continuity, and reproducibility while reducing the amount of institutional knowledge required to understand prior work.
