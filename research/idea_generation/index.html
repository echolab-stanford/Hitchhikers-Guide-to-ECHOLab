    Research Idea Generation#

    Overview#

    Generating research ideas is often one of the least structured parts of the research process. While there is no formula for identifying important questions, it can be helpful to evaluate ideas systematically.

    The goal is not simply to identify interesting topics, but to identify projects that are likely to succeed and have meaningful scientific or real-world impact.

    From Topics to Research Questions#

    Many research ideas begin as broad topics:

    • Climate change and migration
    • Air pollution and health
    • Wildfire smoke
    • Artificial intelligence and education

    However, topics are not research questions.

    A useful research question should be specific enough that it can be answered empirically.

    For example:

    Topic

    Climate change and migration

    Research question

    How do droughts affect rural-to-urban migration in sub-Saharan Africa?

    Testable hypothesis

    Areas experiencing severe drought will experience increased out-migration relative to comparable areas not experiencing drought.

    A useful exercise is to ask:

    • What exactly am I trying to learn?
    • What comparison is being made?
    • What evidence would support or refute the hypothesis?

    If a question cannot be translated into a testable hypothesis, it may still be too broad.

    Four Dimensions of a Strong Research Question#

    Importance#

    Does answering the question matter?

    • Would it change how people think?
    • Would it influence policy, practice, or future research?
    • What decisions could it inform?

    Tractability#

    Can the question actually be answered?

    • Are suitable data available?
    • Is there a plausible empirical strategy?
    • Can the project be completed with available resources?

    Interest#

    Will people care about the answer?

    • Is the question intellectually engaging?
    • Will others find the answer informative or surprising?
    • Is there a compelling story to tell?

    Comparative Advantage#

    Why are we well positioned to answer this question?

    • Do we have access to unique data?
    • Do we have specialized expertise?
    • Do we have collaborators or resources that make the project feasible?

    Favor Answer-Invariant Questions#

    One useful principle is:

    The best research questions remain interesting regardless of the answer.

    A useful test is:

    Would this project still be worth publishing if the main result were null?

    Strong research questions reduce uncertainty rather than confirm expectations.

    Consider the End Product#

    Before investing substantial effort in a project, it can be useful to think about what a successful final paper would look like.

    Questions to consider:

    • What is the main contribution?
    • Who would care about the results?
    • What decisions could the findings inform?
    • What journal or audience might be interested?

    One useful exercise is to draft a hypothetical abstract for the completed paper. The goal is not to predict the results, but to clarify the project's framing, contribution, and potential impact.

    Final Questions#

    Before committing substantial effort to a project, it can be useful to ask:

    • Is the question clearly defined?
    • Can it be translated into a testable hypothesis?
    • Is it important?
    • Is it tractable?
    • Is it interesting?
    • Do we have a comparative advantage?
    • Would it still be interesting if the answer were null?
    • What decisions could the findings inform?

    Projects that score highly across these dimensions are often the most rewarding to pursue.
