    Submitting a Scientific Paper#

    Overview#

    Submitting a paper is often less complicated than it first appears. While requirements vary by journal and field, most submissions follow a similar process.

    This guide is written primarily from the perspective of environmental health, epidemiology, environmental science, and related fields, though many of the concepts apply more broadly.

    1. Choose a Journal#

    Consider:

    • Scope and audience
    • Journal reputation and readership
    • Review timelines
    • Open access requirements
    • Publication costs

    There is rarely a single correct choice. Journal selection often involves balancing audience, impact, speed, and cost.

    2. Prepare Submission Materials#

    In addition to the manuscript, journals often require:

    • Cover letter
    • Supplementary materials
    • Data and code availability statements
    • Conflict of interest disclosures
    • Funding acknowledgments

    Always review the journal's author guidelines before submission.

    3. Write a Cover Letter#

    Most cover letters are short.

    Typically include:

    • The manuscript title
    • The research question
    • Main findings
    • Why the paper fits the journal

    In many fields, cover letters play a relatively minor role in editorial decisions.

    4. Submit the Paper#

    Most journals use online submission systems.

    Typical steps include:

    • Upload files
    • Enter author information
    • Complete disclosure forms
    • Suggest reviewers (if requested)

    The process often takes longer than expected, particularly for large author teams.

    5. What Happens Next?#

    After submission, manuscripts typically undergo:

    1. Editorial screening
    2. Peer review
    3. Editorial decision

    Possible outcomes include:

    • Minor revision
    • Major revision
    • Reject and resubmit
    • Reject

    Many journals reject a substantial fraction of papers before external review. A desk rejection is a normal part of the publication process and often reflects journal fit rather than a fundamental flaw in the science.

    6. Publication Fees#

    Publication costs vary substantially across journals.

    Common models include:

    • No publication fee
    • Optional open access fee
    • Mandatory open access fee

    Fees are typically paid after acceptance, not at submission.

    7. Working Papers and Preprints#

    Many researchers choose to make manuscripts publicly available before journal publication.

    Potential benefits include:

    • Earlier dissemination of findings
    • Increased visibility and citations
    • Opportunities for feedback before publication
    • A public record of the work

    Potential drawbacks include:

    • Public discussion of findings before peer review
    • Additional effort to update public versions
    • Concerns about being scooped in some fields

    Norms vary across disciplines. In environmental health, environmental science, economics, and many related fields, public posting of working papers or preprints is common and often encouraged.

    Examples include:

    • EarthArXiv
    • medRxiv
    • bioRxiv
    • SocArXiv
    • SSRN
    • NBER Working Papers

    Researchers should verify that a target journal permits preprint posting, although many journals now do.

    8. Responding to Reviews#

    When preparing a revision:

    • Respond to every comment.
    • Be respectful and professional.
    • Explain changes clearly.
    • Justify disagreements when necessary.

    A detailed response document is often as important as the manuscript revisions themselves.

    Final Thought#

    Most researchers learn the publication process through experience. When in doubt, consult the journal's author guidelines and ask colleagues who have submitted to the journal before.
